This project was the culmination of Goffredo Puccetti's Wayfinding course, the first University-level interdisciplinary course on Wayfinding Design. Inspired by the Wayfinding design strategies of David Gibson, Yo Kaminagai, and Andrew Mead, we used ethnographic research methods to map the flow of the school library and develop high-quality signage and interior design solutions to address student frustration with noise levels and room capacity.

How can we direct students to the right place to study?


The library flow is confusing


Students percieve no capacity for silent study.

We conducted user interviews, anonymous surveys, and observed social media posts. We identified two major findings that directed our research question.


In our pitch, we presented our research to argue for the importance of a new Wayfinding system that would directly address these findings.

Directional Signage

Our first intervention was to present directional signage that would fit seamlessly into the library's design and encourage students to find a space that matched their desired noise-level. We were inspired by Andrew Mead's design of the Dubai metro system, which employed an artistic flair and an integration with architecture. The result is a system of pillar-based vinyl navigational signs that play on the more colorful school palettes for the downstairs navigators. As students moved further away from the entrance, the signs adopt the more muted library palette to indicate reflection and quiet.

Regulatory Signage

Research showed that nobody understood the capacity rules of study rooms, resulting in their very limited usage. We quickly realized that the existing signage did not adequately convey the "camping" rules in a way students would recognize or feel comfortable acting on. Our intervention inovlves humor and catchy visuals that engage and relate to students in order to normalize the intended usage of these rooms.

Space Segmentation

Our third proposal is proudly being implemented on NYU Abu Dhabi's campus in the 2024-2025 school year. Despite a huge demand for quiet study space, the hundreds of upstairs desks are hardly ever occupied. To students, these spaces seem exposed and hostile to comfortable study. Our simple solution is to add partial barriers that provide a semi-private space for students to feel more comfortable in. These latticed walls can be made from reclaimed materials in the campus woodshop, making the spaces much more comfortable.
