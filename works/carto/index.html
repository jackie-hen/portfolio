SCROLL

Goal

To design something that simplifies navigation for people using public transit, both online and offline. Many users find metro and transit maps difficult to interpret and adapt to, especially when navigating complex routes or in areas with limited connectivity. My app aims to connect different versions of a public transit map, providing a clear and intuitive way for users to understand their route and navigate obstacles.

Core features

1. Navigate seamlessly across the layers of any transit system.
2. Real-time updates about obstacles
3. Offline functionality – access that navigation anywhere

In the beginning

In Spring 2024, I attended the Transit Mapping Symposium in Abu Dhabi, which welcomed mapmakers, designers, commissioners, academics, and fans from across the world to discuss mapmaking strategies and ethics. Panelists and speakers inspired me to think about networks - how they exist only in so far as they are represented in its totality on a map.
There were some questions left unanswered by designers and scholars at the conference:
1. Exhaustiveness or selectiveness in the contents,
2. A general improvement to do for the maps about hubs and stations districts (vs. networks), where passengers’ painpoints are concentrated, and where the z dimension (verticality) interferes the most with the capacities of representations of the users,
3. Continuity of the signs throughout the different media where they are used (printed maps, signage, digital displays in apps, websites and screens, …). Furthermore, the signs on the maps should be coherent with signage (also in the public spaces outside the transport realms), architecture (for example with dissymmetrical spaces or visual perspectives in the stations, which facilitate localization and positioning), and even artworks, which can directly facilitate the identification of a place, of a station, by creating a specificity The HK case has highlighted the role of artworks in giving a specific identity, making their stations more instinctively recognisable.

Process
people to thing

Concept Discovery Interviews

To kick it off, I formalized early conversations around public transit, hoping to find a place to intervene as a designer. I had 15 short interviews with people from medium cities, like Madison and Tampa, and big cities like New York, Mumbai, and Dubai. From these interviews, I extracted pain points and mapped their transit preference to different topics they cared about. The idea for a map integration app emerged from two themes. A common theme was complexity - several people described feeling stressed interpreting static maps. A friction point is navigating transfers and service disruptions. A similar theme was the frustration with offline navigation. People expressed a desire to feel in control during long transits between cities or underground.

Turning this research into two personas helped concentrate my efforts on who Carto can best serve.

Competitive analysis

I analyzed existing transit apps (e.g., Google Maps, Citymapper, local metro apps) to identify areas for improvement. While many apps offered basic routing, they often lacked visual clarity and flexibility for offline use. My focus was on creating an app that integrated various views and versions of transit maps to cater to different user needs in real-time and in areas with poor connectivity.

DEFINING REQUIREMENTS

User Personas & Scenarios

Based on user feedback, I created two personas to capture the needs of different transit riders. For example: Persona 1: Alex, a daily commuter who needs to quickly navigate to work without delay. Persona 2: Maria, a tourist unfamiliar with the city's transit system who needs clear, easy-to-understand directions. Each persona's scenarios focused on understanding their pain points: how to easily find their route, interpret the map, and manage unexpected obstacles. Information Architecture: I mapped out the app’s information architecture, ensuring that it catered to users both online and offline. The core features included: A dynamic, zoomable transit map that connects multiple views (station-level, route-level, city-wide). Real-time updates about service interruptions, delays, or station closures. Offline map functionality, where users could still access key route information without needing an active internet connection. I built the information architecture off user potential user flows.

Usability Testing

Usability Testing

I conducted several rounds of task-based usability testing with public transit users to identify how well the app met user needs. Key tests focused on: Route comprehension: Was it clear where users should go next? Offline functionality: Can users navigate smoothly given obstacles or intrusions? Feedback indicated that users appreciated the simplicity of the map, but some struggled to find stations from the overview map. Based on this, I iterated on the design by emphasizing the search function, making it a more obvious starting place for users looking to jump to stations.

Visual design

UI Design

For the final UI design, I focused on a clean, minimalistic aesthetic that prioritized legibility and ease of navigation. I used high-contrast colors for major transit lines and clear, simple icons for stations and transfer points. Each map view was designed with a clear visual hierarchy to guide the user’s attention to important information, like upcoming stops and detours.
