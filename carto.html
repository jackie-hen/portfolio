CARTO

The goal of this project was to design an app that simplifies navigation for people using public transit, both online and offline. Many metro and transit maps are notoriously difficult to interpret, especially when navigating complex routes or in areas with limited connectivity. My app aims to connect different versions of a public transit map, providing a clear and intuitive way for users to understand their route and navigate potential obstacles, like train delays or station closures.

SCROLL

Research & Discovery

User Research: I began the project by interviewing frequent public transit users to understand the pain points they faced when using traditional metro maps and navigation tools. The majority of users expressed frustration with the difficulty of interpreting complex, static maps—especially in stressful situations like navigating transfers or during service disruptions. Key insights included confusion around map legends, unclear transfer points, and difficulty when trying to navigate offline. Competitive Analysis: I analyzed existing transit apps (e.g., Google Maps, Citymapper, local metro apps) to identify areas for improvement. While many apps offered basic routing, they often lacked visual clarity and flexibility for offline use. My focus was on creating an app that integrated various views and versions of transit maps to cater to different user needs in real-time and in areas with poor connectivity.

DEFINING REQUIREMENTS

User Personas & Scenarios: Based on user feedback, I created two personas to capture the needs of different transit riders. For example: Persona 1: Alex, a daily commuter who needs to quickly navigate to work without delay. Persona 2: Maria, a tourist unfamiliar with the city's transit system who needs clear, easy-to-understand directions. Each persona's scenarios focused on understanding their pain points: how to easily find their route, interpret the map, and manage unexpected obstacles. Information Architecture: I mapped out the app’s information architecture, ensuring that it catered to users both online and offline. The core features included: A dynamic, zoomable transit map that connects multiple views (station-level, route-level, city-wide). Real-time updates about service interruptions, delays, or station closures. Offline map functionality, where users could still access key route information without needing an active internet connection. I built the information architecture off user potential user flows.

Usability Testing

Usability Testing: I conducted several rounds of task-based usability testing with public transit users to identify how well the app met user needs. Key tests focused on: Route comprehension: Was it clear where users should go next? Offline functionality: Can users navigate smoothly given obstacles or intrusions? Feedback indicated that users appreciated the simplicity of the map, but some struggled to find stations from the overview map. Based on this, I iterated on the design by emphasizing the search function, making it a more obvious starting place for users looking to jump to stations

Visual design

UI Design: For the final UI design, I focused on a clean, minimalistic aesthetic that prioritized legibility and ease of navigation. I used high-contrast colors for major transit lines and clear, simple icons for stations and transfer points. Each map view was designed with a clear visual hierarchy to guide the user’s attention to important information, like upcoming stops and detours. Responsive Design: The app was designed to work across both smartphones and tablets, with a responsive layout that adapted seamlessly to different screen sizes. I also designed an intuitive offline mode where users could continue viewing their route and maps even without an active internet connection, with visual cues showing when they were in offline mode.
